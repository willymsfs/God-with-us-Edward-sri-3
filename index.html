God With Us

A Teacher's Guide to the Gospel of Matthew

An immersive journey based on the scholarship of Edward Sri

Our Journey Begins: Finding the Right Glasses

Why We Must Learn to See Jesus as a First-Century Jew

Welcome, students, to our exploration of Matthew's Gospel. Before we open to the first page, we have to ask a critical question: what glasses are we wearing? If we read this ancient text with 21st-century eyes, the story will seem flat, some parts will feel strange, and the true genius will be lost. Our goal is to take off our modern glasses and put on a pair of first-century Jewish ones. Why? Because Matthew was writing for them, and he assumed they knew the whole backstory—the epic tale of Israel.

The central theme, our guiding star for this entire journey, is the name Matthew gives to Jesus: Emmanuel, which means "God with us." This isn't just a beautiful title; it's a mission statement. Jesus's entire life was dedicated to closing the chasm that sin had opened between God and humanity. He came to be God *with* us, so we could once again be with God.

To see this, we must recognize Jesus as the Messiah-King. Forget the gentle, stained-glass-window Jesus for a moment. Think of a long-lost king, the heir to a legendary dynasty, returning to a nation living under bitter occupation. He has come to reclaim his throne and establish his kingdom. This is the electrifying drama Matthew unfolds. He is a meticulous teacher, carefully showing how Jesus is the stunning fulfillment of every hope, every prophecy, and every story his audience held dear.

Part I: The King's Identity and Mission

Chapter 1: The Return of the King (Matthew 1 )

Decoding the Royal Birth Certificate

Alright class, let's be honest. When you see a long list of "begats," your eyes glaze over. You're tempted to skip to the action, right? But for Matthew's audience, this genealogy was the action. It was the front-page, headline news they had been waiting centuries for. Let's break down why this "boring" list is actually a royal birth certificate with explosive implications.

First, Matthew declares Jesus is the "Son of David." This is his thesis statement. But he doesn't just say it; he proves it with a hidden code. He arranges the names into three sets of fourteen generations. This isn't a coincidence. In the Hebrew alphabet, letters have numerical values. The name "David" (D-W-D in Hebrew) adds up to 14 (D=4, W=6, D=4). It's a brilliant, subtle way of shouting from the rooftops: "This Jesus is David, David, David! He is the ultimate king!"

Now, notice the pivot point in the list: the Babylonian Exile. This was the moment the kingdom died, the moment the line of Davidic kings was cut off. For the Jews, this wasn't just history; it was a present reality of pain and humiliation under Roman rule. They felt like a people still in exile. The genealogy builds this historical tension, this memory of loss, and then resolves it in one name: Jesus, the "Christ"—the anointed Messiah. His very name, *Yeshua*, means "God saves." He is the answer to the exile, because he has come to save his people from the sin that caused it in the first place. He is the king who has come to finally bring them home.

Teacher's Analogy: The Broken Crown

Imagine a museum displaying a magnificent royal crown, but with a large piece missing. The plaque explains that the piece was lost when the kingdom fell centuries ago. For generations, people have stared at that gap, a symbol of their lost glory. Then one day, a master jeweler arrives, not with a replica, but with the original, long-lost piece. He fits it into place, and the crown is whole again. The genealogy is that story. The exile is the broken piece. Jesus is the one who makes the royal line—and the hopes of his people—whole again.

Chapter 2: Fulfilling All Prophecy (Matthew 2)

When History Rhymes: Finding the Divine Pattern

If Chapter 1 was the legal document, Chapter 2 is the historical proof. Matthew continues to build his case, showing that Jesus's early life wasn't a series of random events, but a divine pattern, a rhyme with Israel's sacred history. He uses a teaching method called typology, where a person or event in the Old Testament serves as a "type" or a blueprint for something Jesus fulfills.

Think of it like a movie "remake." The plot is familiar, but the new version is grander, deeper, and reveals the ultimate meaning of the original. Matthew presents two major "remakes" here:

  • Jesus as the New Moses: Let's look at the parallels. A wicked, paranoid ruler (Pharaoh/Herod) orders the slaughter of innocent Hebrew boys. A child, destined to be a savior, miraculously escapes. He finds refuge in Egypt. Later, he will return to liberate his people. This is the story of Moses, and Matthew shows it is also the story of Jesus. He is signaling to us: if Moses was the great liberator and lawgiver, then Jesus is the *greater* one. He will lead a new, more profound Exodus—not from bondage in Egypt, but from bondage to sin and death.
  • The Magi as the New Balaam: This one is more subtle, but even more brilliant. In the Old Testament (Numbers 22-24), a pagan king, Balak, hires a seer from the East named Balaam to curse Israel. But God foils the plan, and Balaam ends up blessing Israel and prophesying that "a star shall come out of Jacob" to signal a future king. Now, fast forward to Matthew 2. A new pagan king, Herod, tries to use seers from the East (the Magi) for his own evil ends. But once again, God foils the plan. The Magi, guided by a star, end up blessing and worshipping the true King. The echo would have been unmistakable to Matthew's audience.

Through these echoes, Matthew teaches a profound truth: God is the author of history, and all the old stories were just foreshadowing the main event—the arrival of His Son.

Chapter 3: The King's Anointing (Matthew 3)

A Coronation in the River

So, our King has been identified. What happens next in any royal story? The coronation! But Jesus's coronation doesn't happen in a palace. It happens in the muddy waters of the Jordan River. Why there? Because for Israel, this place was sacred ground, charged with meaning. The Jordan was the final barrier they crossed to enter the Promised Land. It was the symbol of a new beginning. John the Baptist, dressed exactly like the great prophet Elijah (fulfilling the prophecy that Elijah would return to announce the Messiah), is calling Israel to a New Exodus. He's telling them to come back to the beginning and get ready for a new liberation.

When Jesus, the sinless one, enters the water, it's not for his own cleansing. He is identifying with his people. And then, the heavens rip open. The Holy Spirit descends like a dove—not just a gentle bird, but a symbol of the Spirit of God that rushed upon the kings of old at their anointing. The Father's voice booms, "This is my beloved Son." This is the moment of anointing. This is Jesus's official, public inauguration as the Messiah-King, empowered by the Spirit for the mission that lies ahead.

Chapter 4: Battle in the Desert (Matthew 4)

The King's First Battle

What is the first thing a newly crowned king does? He confronts his kingdom's greatest enemy. But who was Israel's true enemy? Many Jews in Jesus's day would have shouted "Rome!" But Jesus knew better. The real enemy wasn't a foreign army, but the ancient, spiritual force of evil that had enslaved humanity from the beginning: Satan. So, Jesus marches into the desert, the domain of demons, to fight his first battle.

This story is another brilliant "remake." Jesus, the New Israel, relives the 40 years his people spent in the desert in 40 days. He faces the same three categories of temptation that caused Israel to fail:

  1. The Test of Flesh (Bread): Israel grumbled and demanded food, failing to trust God. Jesus, though starving, trusts in God's word over bread.
  2. The Test of Faith (Testing God): Israel put God on trial at Massah, demanding a sign. Jesus refuses to test his Father's love.
  3. The Test of Loyalty (Idolatry): Israel bowed to a golden calf. Jesus refuses to bow to anyone but God.

Where the old Israel failed, the New Israel—Jesus—is perfectly faithful. He "unties the knot" of their disobedience. This isn't just a private spiritual test; it is a public victory that prefigures his ultimate triumph on the Cross. He proves he is the champion who can truly liberate his people.

Part II: The Kingdom Proclaimed

Chapter 5: The Challenge of the Kingdom (Matthew 5-7)

The Constitution of an Upside-Down Kingdom

Imagine the scene. Crowds are flocking to this new king. They are expecting a fiery speech, a call to arms, a plan to drive out the Romans. Instead, Jesus leads them up a mountain, like a New Moses on a New Sinai, and delivers the constitution for his kingdom: the Sermon on the Mount. And it is utterly shocking.

He says, "Blessed are the poor in spirit... the meek... the merciful... the peacemakers." This was the opposite of a revolutionary war cry. To a people who felt oppressed and longed for vengeance, Jesus's words were deeply counter-cultural. "Love your enemy"? For them, this meant "Love the Roman soldier who occupies your land and extorts your family." "Turn the other cheek"? This was a call to absorb injustice with love, not to return it with violence.

What was Jesus doing? He was calling Israel back to its original vocation. They were meant to be a "light to the nations," a priestly people who would draw the whole world to God. But they had become a fortress, focused on separating themselves (the Pharisees) or on violent rebellion (the Revolutionaries). Jesus shatters these options and presents a third way: a kingdom of radical love that transforms the world from the inside out.

Teacher's Analogy: A Doctor's Prescription

Israel was sick with the "disease" of sin, and its symptoms were oppression and hatred. The revolutionaries wanted to treat the symptom by fighting Rome. The Pharisees wanted to quarantine the patient. Jesus, the Divine Physician, correctly diagnoses the disease itself. The Sermon on the Mount is his radical prescription: not to fight or to flee, but to heal the world through a shocking dose of mercy, forgiveness, and sacrificial love.

Chapter 6: Speaking Louder than Words (Matthew 8-9)

When Miracles are Living Parables

After delivering his great sermon, Jesus comes down the mountain and puts his words into action. He performs ten miracles, and we must see them not as random acts of kindness, but as powerful, symbolic lessons—living parables that shout his message.

When he heals the blind, the lame, and the deaf, he is directly fulfilling Isaiah's prophecies (Isaiah 35) about the dawning of the messianic age. These are prophetic signs that the kingdom is here! But he goes further. He touches a leper and heals a hemorrhaging woman—people considered ritually "unclean," outcasts from society. In that culture, touching them would make *you* unclean. But Jesus's holiness is so powerful that the opposite happens: his cleanness flows out and makes *them* clean. This is a stunning demonstration that his kingdom is for restoring the outcasts.

Then comes the most provocative act: he forgives the sins of a paralyzed man. The scribes are horrified. Why? Because only God can forgive sins, and He did so through the rituals of the Jerusalem Temple. By forgiving sins on his own authority, Jesus is making a radical claim: "I am the New Temple. The place where humanity meets God and finds forgiveness is no longer a building. It's me." Finally, by eating with sinners, he shows that his kingdom is a hospital for the spiritually sick, and he, the physician, must be with his patients to heal them.

Chapter 7: Conflict in Galilee (Matthew 10-12)

The King Forms His New Royal Council

As Jesus's message becomes clearer, the opposition hardens. The religious leaders, the Pharisees, cannot deny his power, so they resort to the most terrible accusation imaginable: "He casts out demons by the prince of demons." They attribute the work of the Holy Spirit to Satan. Jesus calls this the unforgivable sin—not because God's mercy has a limit, but because it represents a person so completely closing their heart to the truth that they call good "evil." They are cutting themselves off from the very source of forgiveness.

Seeing that the current "shepherds" of Israel are leading the people astray, Jesus takes a monumental step. He chooses twelve men from his followers. Why twelve? Every Jew knew that number represented the twelve tribes of Israel, the foundation of their nation. This was a profoundly symbolic act. Jesus was declaring the formation of a New Israel, a restored people, with himself as the king and these twelve men as his royal council, his new leadership. He gives them his own apostolic authority to preach and heal, binding them to himself. He is building his Church.

Part III: The Mystery of the Kingdom and the King

Chapter 8: The Surprise of the Parables (Matthew 13)

A Veiled Message for a Divided Audience

Right after the conflict with the Pharisees, Jesus dramatically changes his teaching style. He starts speaking in parables. Why? We often think parables make things simpler, but Jesus says the opposite. He tells his disciples, "To you it has been given to know the secrets of the kingdom of heaven, but to them it has not been given."

Parables are a form of spiritual triage. They are cryptic stories that act like a veil. For those with open hearts and a genuine desire to understand (the disciples), the veil is thin, and they can ask for the deeper meaning. But for those with hardened hearts (like the Pharisees and the unrepentant crowds), the story remains just a story, concealing the treasure within. The Parable of the Sower is the key to understanding this. The sower is Jesus, and the seed is his word. The different soils represent the state of people's hearts. The Pharisees are the hardened path where the seed can't penetrate. The excitable but shallow crowds are the rocky ground. The worldly and anxious are the thorny soil. Only the disciples are the good soil, ready to receive the word and bear fruit.

Chapter 9: The Keys to the Kingdom (Matthew 14:1-16:19)

The King Appoints His Prime Minister

This chapter brings us to a pivotal moment at Caesarea Philippi. Jesus asks his disciples, "Who do you say that I am?" Simon answers for them all with the great confession: "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God." In response, Jesus gives Simon a new name: Peter, which means "Rock." In Jewish tradition, a new name from God means a new mission. Peter is to be the rock, the foundation, upon which Jesus will build his Church.

This "rock" imagery alludes to the foundation stone of the Temple, which was believed to hold back the "gates of hell"—the powers of death and chaos. Peter is to be the stabilizing force for the Church on earth. Then, Jesus gives him the "keys of the kingdom." This is a direct reference to Isaiah 22, where the Davidic king gives the keys of his palace to his prime minister, an official who governs with the king's own authority. Jesus, the new Davidic King, is appointing Peter as his royal prime minister. And since this was a continuing office in the old kingdom, it implies succession. Jesus was establishing an enduring office of leadership for his Church on earth.

The Keys represent divine authority to govern; the Rock represents an unshakeable foundation against the forces of chaos.

Chapter 10: The Secret of the Messiah (Matthew 16:20-21:11 )

The Shocking Path to the Throne

The disciples are ecstatic. Their leader is the Messiah! They are likely dreaming of cabinet positions in the new kingdom. But Jesus immediately shatters their expectations. He reveals the great "messianic secret": he must be a suffering Messiah. He predicts his own passion, death, and resurrection. Peter, the newly appointed prime minister, is so horrified by this that he rebukes Jesus! The idea of a suffering king was unthinkable.

Jesus then issues the radical call of discipleship: "Whoever wishes to come after me must deny himself, take up his cross, and follow me." He transforms the cross, a Roman symbol of shameful torture, into the emblem of his kingdom. To prepare them for this shocking reality, he takes his inner circle—Peter, James, and John—up a mountain for the Transfiguration, giving them a glimpse of his divine glory to strengthen their faith. Finally, his royal entry into Jerusalem on a donkey is a public declaration of his kingship, but it fulfills Zechariah's prophecy of a humble king who comes in peace, not a warrior king coming for war.

Part IV: The Climax of the Kingdom

Chapter 11: Jesus, the Temple, and the End Times (Matthew 21:12-25:46)

The King Judges His Own House

Upon entering Jerusalem, the King goes to his palace: the Temple. But instead of worshipping, he performs an act of prophetic judgment. His cleansing of the Temple—overturning the tables—is a symbolic act that temporarily shuts down the entire sacrificial system. He condemns the Temple leadership for turning his Father's "house of prayer for all nations" into a "den of robbers"—not just a place of commerce, but a hotbed of nationalistic, revolutionary zeal that excluded the very Gentiles it was meant to welcome.

Later, Jesus predicts the Temple's utter destruction, using dramatic apocalyptic language like the sun darkening and stars falling. His disciples think he's talking about the end of the world. But this was standard Old Testament prophetic language for the fall of a corrupt regime or nation. Jesus was predicting the end of the *Old Covenant world*, which was centered on the Temple. And he gives a specific timeframe: "this generation." This prophecy was stunningly fulfilled in AD 70 when the Roman armies destroyed Jerusalem and the Temple, marking the definitive end of that age.

Chapter 12: The Trial of the King (Matthew 26:1-27:26)

The Accused Becomes the Judge

At his trial before the Sanhedrin, the King appears to be a helpless victim. But with two short sentences, he seizes control and puts his accusers on trial. When asked if he is the Messiah, he replies, "You have said so. But I tell you, from now on you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power and coming on the clouds of heaven."

This is a direct reference to Daniel 7, a vision every Jewish leader knew well. In it, the "Son of Man" (representing God's faithful people) is persecuted by four monstrous beasts (pagan empires). But in the end, God vindicates the Son of Man and gives him everlasting dominion. By claiming this title, Jesus casts himself as the true representative of God's people and, in a shocking twist, casts the Jewish leaders in the role of the beasts—the enemies of God's people. The final choice offered to the crowd—Jesus or Barabbas (a revolutionary)—becomes a symbolic choice for the nation: will you follow the way of the Cross, or the way of violent rebellion? They choose rebellion, and seal their fate.

Chapter 13: The Climax of the Cross (Matthew 27:27-66)

The Throne of Victory

Here we arrive at the climax of all history. The Cross is not a tragedy; it is a triumph. It is the King's throne. At the Last Supper, Jesus had already interpreted his death for us: he is the New Passover Lamb, whose sacrificial death establishes a New Covenant and liberates his people from the slavery of sin. On the Cross, he enters into complete solidarity with sinners, taking upon himself the curse of death that resulted from Israel's (and all humanity's) covenant unfaithfulness. By uniting himself to us in our lowest point, he can lift us up with him in his Resurrection.

This event is the ultimate fulfillment of the sacrifice of Isaac. On that same mountain range, Abraham was willing to offer his beloved son. Now, God the Father offers His beloved Son. Isaac carried the wood for his own sacrifice; Jesus carries the wood of the Cross. But this time, the sacrifice is not stopped. It is completed, and through it, the blessing promised to Abraham finally flows out to all the nations of the world. This is not the defeat of a victim; it is the enthronement of a victorious King.

Chapter 14: Great Commission (Matthew 28)

A Kingdom Without Borders

Our journey ends where the Church's mission begins. The resurrected Jesus appears to his disciples. The Son of Man who was persecuted has now, as Daniel prophesied, been given all authority in heaven and on earth. His first royal decree is the Great Commission. The kingdom, once focused on Israel, now shatters all borders and extends to "all nations." The apostles, his royal council, are sent out as ambassadors to bring the whole world into his kingdom.

And he ends with the promise that started it all, the theme that has echoed through every chapter: Still Emmanuel. "And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age." This is the promise that fuels the Church. The King has won the decisive victory, and now he sends us out to share in that victory, promising his own divine presence as our constant companion and our ultimate hope.

Our Final Lesson: Living as Citizens of the Kingdom

What the Author Wants Us to Take Away from This Study

Class, we have traveled from the royal genealogy to the empty tomb. We have seen Jesus as the New Moses, the New David, the true Temple, and the ultimate Passover Lamb. So, what now? What is the final lesson Edward Sri, through his deep reading of Matthew, wants us to carry out of this classroom and into our lives? It is not simply to be impressed by the theology, but to be transformed by the King.

The Teacher's Final Exam: Four Core Truths to Live By

  • Become a Biblical Detective: The first lesson is a call to intellectual conversion. We can no longer be passive readers of the Bible. Sri teaches us that the Old and New Testaments are a divine dialogue. To understand Jesus, we must understand the promises he came to fulfill. The challenge is to become "biblical detectives," constantly asking, "What Old Testament story is echoing here? What prophecy is being fulfilled?" This approach turns Bible reading from a duty into a lifelong adventure of discovery.
  • Understand True Power: Our world defines power as domination, wealth, and influence. Jesus's kingdom turns this upside down. True power, as Sri shows through Matthew, is found in humility, service, and sacrificial love. The Sermon on the Mount is the constitution of this new kingdom. The Cross is its throne. The lesson for us is to re-evaluate our own ambitions. Are we seeking to "lord it over" others in our work or family, or are we seeking to be the "servant of all"? This is the daily choice of a kingdom citizen.
  • Your Life is a Mission, Not a Spectacle: The Great Commission at the end of Matthew is not just a historical command; it is our personal marching order. Sri wants us to realize that we are not spectators watching the story of Jesus from the stands. We have been drafted onto the team. Our mission is to extend the borders of his kingdom of light and love in our own small corners of the world. Every act of kindness, every word of truth, every prayer for an enemy is an act of building the kingdom.
  • Live in the Reality of "Emmanuel": This is the most important lesson of all, the one that makes all the others possible. The promise of "God with us" is not a sentimental platitude. It is the source of all Christian power and joy. Sri emphasizes that this presence is real and accessible. When we feel weak, we can turn to Christ in the Eucharist. When we feel lost, we can hear his voice in Scripture. When we feel alone, we can find him in the community of the Church. The ultimate lesson is to live with the profound, unshakeable confidence that the King is with us, always, to the very end of the age.

Thank you for taking this journey. The goal was never just to understand the Gospel of Matthew, but to allow the Gospel of Matthew to understand us, and to reshape us into more faithful and courageous citizens of the kingdom of "God with us."
